Overview

Course description

Why is human language the way that it is? This course will examine how human language is shaped by “nature” (the biological and genetic characteristics of human beings) as well as by “nurture” (the cultural and social contexts in which humans develop, learn, and use language). We will examine the linguistic structure of natural languages, both spoken and signed, to identify the ways in which languages differ from one another—and the ways in which they do not. To understand how language came to be this way, we will ask which aspects of this (in)variability are present in the rudimentary communication systems developed by Deaf children who grow up without exposure to a natural signed language; in new or emerging human languages (e.g., Nicaraguan Sign Language, Amami Island Sign Language); and in animal communication systems. We will also examine how the process of cultural transmission influences language structure, using evidence from the development of real languages and also from psychological experiments on language evolution in the lab. A major component of the course is our “Grow a Language” project, in which students construct an artificial language and study how the language changes as it is transmitted from one “generation” of learners to the next (a replication of a famous psychological experiment). Through the course, students will gain insight into the deep similarities across languages (including signed languages); the ability to identify and interpret scientific evidence relevant to the nature/nurture question in human cognition; and hands-on experience conducting an experiment in psycholinguistics.

Learning goals

The course is designed to achieve the following learning outcomes:

  • Knowledge of how languages are structured
  • Knowledge of the equality of languages and language varieties and commonly held misconceptions about them (especially signed languages)
  • Knowledge of the complexity and underlying systematicity of natural languages
  • Knowledge of the theories and empirical findings relevant to the nature/nurture question in language structure and acquisition
  • Ability to analyze and interpret linguistic and psycholinguistic data
  • Ability to carry out a psycholinguistic research experiment

Grow a Language project

Throughout the semester, you will work with one or two classmates to study the evolution of a made-up miniature language. Here’s how it works: You’ll start by making up a list of words for your language, and you’ll give each word a meaning. You’ll set up a psycholinguistic language-learning experiment and ask a friend to participate in the experiment and learn what the words mean. At the end of the experiment, you’ll test your friend to see if they learned the words. Then, you’ll use that friend’s responses on the test to form the list of words that your next friend learns from. It’s like a game of telephone. This is a replication of a famous experiment by Dr. Simon Kirby at the University of Edinburgh—we’ll read the study in class. In Prof. Kirby’s original experiments, as the miniature language was transmitted from learner to learner, the language became less arbitrary, and the parts of words started to mean specific things—just like they often do in real languages. In class, you’ll study what happens to your own miniature language and compare it to the original. Through this project, you’ll get hands-on experience running a psycholinguistic experiment and conducting data analysis, and you’ll see how the process of language transmission can shape language structure.

Attendance

Attendance is mandatory. Unexplained absences will affect your grade. That said:

  • If you know in advance that you won’t be able to make it to a particular class due to a religious holiday, sports conflict, etc., no problem—just let me know ahead of time.
  • If you’re sick and/or contagious, please DO NOT come to class. Email me to let me know you are not well enough to come. I will not ask any follow-up questions, and I will never require you to provide a doctor’s note.

There is no Zoom option for this course.

Extensions

For some homeworks, it may be possible to request an extension of up to one week. You don’t have to tell me why. You can request an extension for as many assignments as you need to, but you can’t request more than one extension per assignment. Check with about whether extensions are available for a particular assignment as soon as you recognize that you might need one.

Use of AI

I think that generative AI (Chat GPT, etc.) can be a great tool and that students should learn to use it. However, this course is not the place for you to do that. This course is also not one in which generative AI would do a particularly good job. In any case, I expect you to complete all of your work without the aid of generative AI, unless I specifically say otherwise. In this course, presenting AI-generated work (including text and ideas) as your own constitutes a violation of academic integrity. If I suspect that AI was used for an assignment, I will refer the submission to the Honor Council for investigation. This can have enormous consequences for you, including a permanent notation on your transcript or even suspension or dismissal from Georgetown.

It is ok to use basic copy-editing/spell-checking tools like the ones provided by Grammarly, Microsoft Word, and Google Docs.

Accommodations

If you might require an accommodation in this course, you may contact the Academic Resource Center (ARC). Available accommodations include notetaking assistance, interpreting/CART services, extra time on assignments, or any other accommodation that the ARC deems “reasonable” based on a conversation with you and documentation from the appropriate people (e.g. your doctor). To arrange accommodations (or just to explore this option), email arc@georgetown.edu or visit the ARC in-person (Leavey Center, Suite 335, which is accessible from the elevators next to the student credit union, around the corner from the bookstore). There’s more information about this process on the ARC’s website.

Recording course lectures: One possible ARC accommodation is access to special note-taking software. This software allows students to audio-record class, generate a transcription of that recording, and store it alongside other course materials (e.g., lecture slides). When students are approved for this accommodation, they must sign a document agreeing that recordings are for their personal use only, and they are required to delete all recordings at the end of the semester.
